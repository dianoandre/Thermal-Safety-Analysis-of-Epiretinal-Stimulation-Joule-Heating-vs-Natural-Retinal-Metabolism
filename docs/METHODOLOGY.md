# Methodology and Technical Documentation

## 1. Project Overview
This project presents a Finite Element (FE) analysis, conducted in COMSOL Multiphysics, investigating the thermal safety of epiretinal stimulation. The geometric and functional setup is inspired by the Argus II retinal prosthesis. The fundamental goal is to quantify the Joule heating generated by electrical stimulation and compare it directly to the natural metabolic heat produced by the retinal tissue.

## 2. Clinical Motivation
Retinal prostheses are an advanced neuroprosthetic approach for treating blindness caused by outer retina degenerations, such as Retinitis Pigmentosa and Age-related Macular Degeneration (AMD). These pathologies destroy the photoreceptors but leave the inner neural layers—specifically the Retinal Ganglion Cells (RGCs)—largely intact. The Argus II system bypasses the damaged photoreceptors by electrically stimulating the surviving RGCs via an array of microelectrodes placed on the epiretinal surface.

However, electrical stimulation introduces a potential risk: **thermal damage**. When current passes through the resistive biological tissue, Joule heating occurs at the electrode-tissue interface. According to ISO 14708 standards, active implantable devices must not elevate tissue temperature by more than 1–2 °C. 

To properly contextualize the thermal safety of the implant, we modeled not only the Joule heating but also the intrinsic metabolic heat of the retina. The retina, particularly the photoreceptor layer, is one of the most metabolically active tissues in the human body.

## 3. Retinal Anatomy and Prosthesis Setup
The retina is a stratified neural tissue. The light traverses several layers before reaching the photoreceptors at the back. Underlying the neural retina is the Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE) and the highly vascularized choroid, which acts as the primary heat sink via blood perfusion.

The prosthesis model simplifies the Argus II array into a 3x3 grid of cylindrical platinum electrodes:
*   **Diameter:** 200 µm
*   **Thickness:** 10 µm
*   **Pitch:** 575 µm
*   **Insulation:** A 15 µm polyimide substrate above the electrodes.

The stimulation is applied in an epiretinal configuration, meaning the electrodes are in direct contact with the RGC layer.

## 4. Retinal Metabolic Heat
To quantify the physiological metabolic heat, we referenced in-vivo oxygen consumption studies by Braun & Linsenmeier (cat), Yu & Cringle (rat), and Birol et al. (macaque). Using the caloric equivalent of oxygen ($~20 \text{ kJ} / \text{L O}_2$), the oxygen consumption was converted into volumetric heat source values ($\text{W} / \text{m}^3$).

*   **Outer Nuclear Layer (Photoreceptors):** $10,000 \text{ W} / \text{m}^3$ (highest rate, dark-adapted).
*   **RPE:** $4,000 \text{ W} / \text{m}^3$.
*   **Inner Retina (RGC, INL, Plexiform layers):** $800 - 1,500 \text{ W} / \text{m}^3$. These lower values represent the *net* metabolic heat, implicitly accounting for the cooling effect of the inner retinal capillary network, which was not explicitly modeled with Pennes perfusion.

## 5. Mathematical Model
The core of the model couples two physics:
1.  **Electric Currents (Quasi-static):** Solves the Volume Conductor Theory PDE ($\nabla \cdot (\sigma \nabla\phi) = 0$).
2.  **Heat Transfer in Solids (Pennes Bioheat):** Extends the classic heat diffusion equation with metabolic heat generation and blood perfusion. 
    *   Blood perfusion ($\omega_b = 0.004 \text{ s}^{-1}$) is applied exclusively to the choroid.
    *   **Electromagnetic Heating:** Couples the two physics by calculating the volumetric Joule heating as $Q_{Joule} = \mathbf{J} \cdot \mathbf{E} = \sigma |\nabla \phi|^2$.

### Biphasic Waveform
The stimulation uses a standard biphasic periodic pulse (50 Hz, 20 ms period). Each cycle includes:
*   Cathodic phase (1 ms, $+20\ \mu\text{A}$)
*   Interphase gap (0.1 ms, $0\ \mu\text{A}$)
*   Anodic phase (1 ms, $-20\ \mu\text{A}$)
*   Resting period.
The resulting duty cycle is 10.5%.

## 6. COMSOL Model Setup
The 3D geometry consists of a 3x3 mm block covering all 9 retinal layers (total thickness ~630 µm from vitreous to choroid base). Electrical and thermal properties were derived from Werginz et al. (2015). A significant conductivity gradient exists: the highly conductive vitreous ($1.25 \text{ S/m}$) versus the resistive cellular layers ($\sim 0.01 \text{ S/m}$).

Three studies were configured:
1.  **Joule Only:** Time-dependent over 20 ms (metabolic sources disabled).
2.  **Metabolic Only:** Stationary study providing the natural thermal baseline.
3.  **Combined:** Time-dependent, integrating both heat sources, using the metabolic baseline as the initial condition. 
An Auxiliary Sweep on the polyimide thermal conductivity ($k$) was performed on the Combined study to assess the insulation barrier effect.

## 7. Results

### Joule Only
Joule heating is highly localized at the electrode-tissue interfaces, rapidly decaying with distance due to the $Q_{Joule} \propto |\mathbf{E}|^2$ relationship. The maximum temperature elevation ($\Delta T$) is **~8.88 µK**.

### Metabolic Baseline
The stationary study reveals a maximum $\Delta T$ of **~0.066 °C** (66 mK), peaking at the photoreceptor layer. This aligns perfectly with physiological estimates and represents the natural thermal baseline.

### Combined Study & Comparative $T(z)$
In the combined study, the temperature profile is almost entirely dominated by the metabolic contribution. Plotting $T$ along the depth ($z$):
*   The metabolic curve shows a broad rise peaking in the ONL.
*   The Joule curve shows a sharp, microscopic peak right at the electrode interface.
*   The Combined curve is essentially the linear superposition of the two.

**Crucial Finding:** The $\Delta T$ of the Joule effect is just **0.013%** of the metabolic baseline, rendering it entirely negligible.

### Polyimide Effect
Despite polyimide having a thermal conductivity nearly 5 times lower than tissue, the Auxiliary Sweep showed a temperature difference of only ~1 µK between the insulated and non-insulated scenarios. The theoretical thermal barrier effect is completely masked by the overwhelming dominance of the metabolic heat over the Joule heat.

## 8. Discussion and Conclusions
The generated Joule heat (~9 µK) is four orders of magnitude lower than the natural metabolic baseline (~66 mK). At standard stimulation currents ($20\ \mu\text{A}$), the epiretinal prosthesis is intrinsically safe from a thermal perspective.

**Limitations and Future Work:**
*   Inner retinal capillary perfusion was not explicitly modeled (net heat was used instead).
*   Geometry was simplified to a 3x3 array rather than the full 60-electrode array.
*   Future developments could include parametric sweeps on current amplitude, stationary modeling of long-term chronic average power, and in-vivo validation.
